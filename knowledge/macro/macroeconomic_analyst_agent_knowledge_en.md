# Macroeconomic Analyst Agent Knowledge Base (English)

## 1. Agent purpose

This knowledge base is for an agent that analyzes macroeconomic conditions, with emphasis on Indonesia and on implications for insurance, reinsurance, investment portfolios, pricing assumptions, solvency, and market-update writing.

The agent must be able to:
- Explain macroeconomic concepts clearly.
- Read and summarize macroeconomic policy documents, financial-market updates, government budget material, and insurer/reinsurer management discussion.
- Convert macro indicators into business implications for insurers and reinsurers.
- Build a consistent market narrative from data, policy, and risk signals.
- Separate facts, assumptions, forecasts, and interpretation.

## 2. Source hierarchy

Use this order when answering or drafting analysis:

1. User-provided documents and uploaded project knowledge.
2. Official Indonesian sources: Ministry of Finance, Bank Indonesia, BPS, OJK, LPS, Coordinating Ministry for Economic Affairs, and relevant ministries.
3. Multilateral sources: IMF, World Bank, OECD, ADB, BIS, IEA, FAO, WTO.
4. Market data providers and exchange sources when available.
5. Reputable financial press only as secondary context.

For every forecast or data point, state:
- period,
- source,
- unit,
- whether it is actual, budget assumption, projection, target, or analyst estimate,
- date of publication.

## 3. Core definition of macroeconomics

Macroeconomics studies the economy as a whole. It focuses on aggregate behavior, including aggregate income, general price movements, unemployment, and the interaction of major markets.

A macroeconomic analyst should treat output, unemployment, and the price level as core indicators because they help assess a country's economic performance and the possible implications for households, firms, financial markets, and public policy.

Primary indicators:
- **Real GDP**: total real income/output produced in an economy over a period.
- **Inflation rate**: the pace of increase in the general price level.
- **Unemployment rate**: the share of the labor force that does not have work.

Economists use simplified models to explain relationships among variables. In an economic model:
- **Endogenous variables** are explained by the model.
- **Exogenous variables** are determined outside the model and used as inputs.
- The purpose of a model is to show how exogenous variables influence endogenous outcomes.

## 4. Key markets in macro analysis

Analyze the economy through connected markets, not isolated products.

### 4.1 Labor market

Core logic:
- Households supply labor.
- Firms and government demand labor.
- Wages are the price of labor.
- Labor demand is derived demand from demand for output.
- Equilibrium occurs when labor demand equals labor supply.
- Disequilibrium may appear as unemployment, labor shortage, wage pressure, productivity mismatch, or underemployment.

Insurance relevance:
- Employment growth supports premium affordability and persistency.
- Wage growth can raise operating costs, medical-service costs, and insured values.
- Rising unemployment increases lapse risk and weakens retail insurance demand.

### 4.2 Commodity and goods market

Core logic:
- Firms supply goods and services.
- Households, government, firms, and foreign buyers demand goods and services.
- Output is linked to production inputs, productivity, capacity, inventories, and demand conditions.
- Price flexibility is more useful for long-run analysis; sticky prices and wages are more useful for short-run analysis.

Core expenditure identity:

```text
Y = C + I + G + (X - M)
```

Where:
- **Y** = output or aggregate demand/GDP.
- **C** = household consumption.
- **I** = investment.
- **G** = government spending.
- **X - M** = net exports.

Interpretation:
- Consumption drives household-linked sectors.
- Investment signals future capacity and business confidence.
- Government spending can stabilize demand.
- Net exports reveal external demand, commodity exposure, and exchange-rate sensitivity.

Insurance relevance:
- GDP growth and consumption affect new business premium.
- Investment activity affects engineering, property, credit, marine, liability, and surety lines.
- Commodity prices affect energy, mining, agriculture, transport, and catastrophe exposure.
- Imported inflation and supply-chain disruption can raise claim severity.

### 4.3 Money market

Core logic:
- The money market links money demand, money supply, liquidity, and interest rates.
- Equilibrium interest rate occurs when money demanded equals money supplied.
- When money supply or liquidity conditions change, interest rates and financial asset prices adjust.
- Central-bank policy affects money-market rates, banking liquidity, credit growth, exchange rates, and asset prices.

Insurance relevance:
- Interest rates affect bond valuations, reinvestment yield, discount rates, reserve adequacy, and ALM.
- Liquidity stress affects surrender behavior, asset liquidation risk, and funding conditions.

### 4.4 Capital market

Core logic:
- The capital market matches long-term funding supply and demand.
- Instruments include bonds, equity, sukuk, and other long-term securities.
- It provides long-term financing, investment options, diversification, and signals on economic expectations.

Insurance relevance:
- Insurers are institutional investors; bond yields and equity markets affect investment income and solvency.
- Sovereign yield movements affect asset valuation, liability discounting, and capital adequacy.
- Equity-market drawdowns can weaken unit-linked returns and consumer trust.

### 4.5 External sector

Track:
- Current account.
- Trade balance.
- Exports and imports.
- Capital and financial account.
- Foreign direct investment.
- Portfolio flows.
- International reserves.
- Exchange rate.
- External debt.
- Global commodity prices.
- Global interest rates.
- Trade-policy and geopolitical shocks.

Insurance relevance:
- Rupiah depreciation can raise imported medical equipment, spare parts, building materials, and reinsurance costs.
- External volatility affects investment yields, capital flows, and market sentiment.
- Reinsurance often has foreign-currency exposure; exchange-rate changes can affect ceded premiums, recoveries, and capital planning.

## 5. Fiscal and policy analysis

### 5.1 Fiscal stance

Classify fiscal policy as:
- **Expansionary**: supports demand through higher spending, tax relief, subsidies, or transfers.
- **Contractionary**: restrains demand through lower spending, higher taxes, or tighter financing.
- **Countercyclical**: offsets downturns or overheating.
- **Procyclical**: amplifies the existing cycle.

When evaluating fiscal stance, compare:
- deficit as percent of GDP,
- primary balance,
- debt ratio,
- interest payments,
- revenue ratio,
- tax ratio,
- spending quality,
- subsidy burden,
- social assistance,
- capital expenditure,
- contingent liabilities.

### 5.2 APBN as shock absorber

For Indonesia, analyze APBN as a stabilizer:
- It can protect households and firms from shocks.
- It can preserve purchasing power and market confidence.
- It must remain credible, prudent, and sustainable.
- Deficit financing should be assessed together with debt ratio, SBN yield, currency composition, refinancing risk, and investor base.

### 5.3 Revenue policy

Analyze:
- tax revenue growth versus nominal GDP growth,
- VAT, income tax, customs, excise, and natural-resource revenue,
- non-tax state revenue (PNBP),
- commodity-price sensitivity,
- tax compliance and reform,
- digital economy and base-broadening measures,
- incentives and their fiscal cost.

Insurance relevance:
- Tax and disposable-income changes affect insurance affordability.
- Tax treatment of insurance products can affect demand.
- Sovereign revenue quality affects bond risk premia.

### 5.4 Spending policy

Analyze:
- mandatory spending,
- subsidies and compensation,
- social protection,
- health, education, infrastructure, defense, energy, food security,
- regional transfers,
- spending efficiency and execution rate,
- multiplier effects,
- targeting accuracy.

Insurance relevance:
- Health and social-protection policy can affect private health/life insurance demand.
- Infrastructure spending affects insurable assets and commercial risk.
- Subsidies affect inflation, fuel prices, logistics costs, and claims costs.

### 5.5 Financing policy

Analyze:
- gross and net financing need,
- SBN issuance strategy,
- domestic versus foreign-currency debt,
- fixed versus floating-rate debt,
- refinancing risk,
- investor composition,
- yield curve,
- sovereign rating and market confidence.

Insurance relevance:
- Insurers are major buyers of government securities.
- SBN yields affect investment income and market valuation.
- Sovereign stress can affect solvency, liquidity, and asset allocation.

## 6. Indonesia KEM-PPKF 2026 reference frame

Use the following as a policy reference when analyzing Indonesia's 2026 macro-fiscal outlook.

### 6.1 Strategic theme

The 2026 KEM-PPKF frames macroeconomic and fiscal policy around **food, energy, and economic sovereignty**. It positions fiscal policy to manage global uncertainty while supporting development priorities.

Eight strategic priorities:
1. Food security.
2. Energy security.
3. Free nutritious meals.
4. Education programs.
5. Health programs.
6. Village, cooperative, and MSME development.
7. Whole-of-nation defense.
8. Investment acceleration and global trade.

### 6.2 2026 macro assumptions

| Indicator | 2026 KEM-PPKF range |
|---|---:|
| Real GDP growth | 5.2%-5.8% |
| Inflation | 1.5%-3.5% |
| Exchange rate | Rp16,500-Rp16,900 per USD |
| 10-year SBN yield | 6.6%-7.2% |
| Indonesian Crude-oil Price (ICP) | USD60-USD80 per barrel |
| Oil lifting | 600-605 thousand barrels per day |
| Gas lifting | 953-1,017 thousand barrels of oil equivalent per day |

Analyst interpretation:
- Growth target implies a pro-growth policy setting but still vulnerable to trade, investment, and commodity shocks.
- Inflation target requires coordination between government and Bank Indonesia, especially food and administered prices.
- Exchange-rate range implies continued external pressure and need for credible policy mix.
- SBN yield range is central for insurer investment income, market value of bonds, and ALM.
- ICP and lifting assumptions matter for fiscal revenue, subsidies, trade balance, energy sector activity, and inflation.

### 6.3 2026 macro-fiscal posture

| Indicator | 2026 KEM-PPKF range |
|---|---:|
| Revenue and grants | 11.71%-12.22% of GDP |
| Tax revenue | 10.08%-10.45% of GDP |
| Non-tax revenue | 1.63%-1.76% of GDP |
| State spending | 14.19%-14.75% of GDP |
| Primary balance | -0.18% to -0.22% of GDP |
| Fiscal deficit | 2.48%-2.53% of GDP |
| Financing | 2.48%-2.53% of GDP |
| Debt ratio | 39.69%-39.85% of GDP |

Analyst interpretation:
- Deficit remains below 3% of GDP but is still expansionary.
- Revenue ratio is a structural constraint; analyze revenue mobilization carefully.
- Debt ratio around 40% of GDP requires monitoring of interest-rate, currency, and refinancing risks.
- Primary deficit implies interest burden and financing costs must be watched.

### 6.4 2026 national development targets

| Indicator | 2026 KEM-PPKF target |
|---|---:|
| Extreme poverty | 0% |
| Poverty rate | 6.5%-7.5% |
| Open unemployment rate | 4.44%-4.96% |
| Gini ratio | 0.377-0.380 |
| Human Capital Index | 0.57 |

Analyst interpretation:
- Growth quality matters as much as growth rate.
- Employment, poverty, inequality, and human capital should be included in macro narratives.
- For insurance, rising formal employment and human capital can support market penetration; inequality and weak purchasing power can constrain persistency.

### 6.5 2026 risk map

Key risks to monitor:
- Geopolitical conflict.
- Trade war and protectionism.
- Global fragmentation and supply-chain disruption.
- Commodity-price volatility.
- Exchange-rate pressure.
- Global interest-rate volatility and "higher-for-longer" risk.
- Slower global growth and trade volume.
- Climate change and food security risk.
- Energy-security risk.
- Digital transformation and structural labor-market shifts.
- Human-resource quality and productivity.
- Fiscal risk from macro assumptions, contingent liabilities, program execution, public-sector balance sheet, and regional fiscal conditions.

## 8. Analytical workflow

### 8.1 Daily or weekly macro monitor

1. Collect data.
2. Validate source, timestamp, unit, frequency, and revision status.
3. Compare latest reading with:
   - previous period,
   - consensus if available,
   - government/budget assumption,
   - internal forecast,
   - same period last year,
   - long-run trend.
4. Identify drivers.
5. Identify sector and insurance impacts.
6. Classify the signal:
   - growth,
   - inflation,
   - external,
   - fiscal,
   - monetary,
   - financial-market,
   - labor,
   - social-development,
   - climate/commodity.
7. Create a base case and at least one risk case.
8. State confidence level and next data to watch.

### 8.2 Market update structure

Use this structure for a market update:

1. **Headline**
   - One-sentence conclusion.
2. **Key numbers**
   - Table of latest data and changes.
3. **What changed**
   - Data, policy, market price, or external event.
4. **Why it changed**
   - Drivers and evidence.
5. **So what**
   - Implication for GDP, inflation, interest rates, currency, fiscal balance, and insurance sector.
6. **Risks**
   - Upside and downside scenarios.
7. **Watchlist**
   - Next releases, policy meetings, global events, and market levels.
8. **Recommended action**
   - For investment, ALM, underwriting, pricing, business planning, or communications.

### 8.3 Scenario analysis

Always build scenarios using a clear shock and transmission chain.

Example format:
- **Shock**: Rupiah depreciates 5% beyond base case.
- **First-round impact**: imported inflation, imported claim costs, USD liabilities, reinsurance costs.
- **Second-round impact**: BI policy response, SBN yield pressure, bond valuation, consumer purchasing power.
- **Insurance impact**: higher claims, lower persistency, asset-liability mismatch, capital volatility.
- **Mitigation**: currency matching, reinsurance review, inflation-indexed sums insured, duration management, pricing review.

Minimum scenario set:
- Base case.
- Upside case.
- Downside case.
- Stress case if the decision is high impact.

### 8.4 Stress-test variables for insurance

Use these macro assumptions in stress tests:
- GDP growth.
- Inflation.
- Medical inflation.
- Interest rates and yield curve.
- Exchange rate.
- Equity-market return.
- Credit spreads/default rate.
- Property/construction-cost inflation.
- Commodity prices.
- Unemployment.
- Mortality or morbidity shock if linked to macro-health stress.
- Catastrophe or climate shock if relevant.
- Fiscal or sovereign-spread shock.

## 9. Quality rules

The agent must:
- Distinguish actual data from targets and forecasts.
- Never mix real and nominal growth without labeling.
- Never annualize a number unless the method is stated.
- Never compare month-on-month, quarter-on-quarter, and year-on-year data without explaining frequency.
- Report whether figures are in percent, percentage points, basis points, Rp, USD, index points, or percent of GDP.
- Check whether data are seasonally adjusted.
- Use "upside" and "downside" relative to the base case, not relative to personal opinion.
- Avoid unsupported causal claims.
- Give a confidence level when evidence is incomplete.
- Show formulas for calculations when the result may affect decisions.
- Use source citations for all non-trivial data.

## 10. Common formulas

```text
Nominal growth approx. = Real growth + Inflation
Real interest rate approx. = Nominal interest rate - Inflation
GDP expenditure = C + I + G + (X - M)
Net exports = Exports - Imports
Primary balance = Revenue - Non-interest spending
Fiscal balance = Revenue - Total spending
Debt ratio = Government debt / GDP
Current account = Trade balance + net services + net primary income + net secondary income
NNP = GNP - Depreciation
Disposable income = Personal income - Personal tax
```

## 11. Glossary

- **APBN**: Indonesia state budget.
- **BI-Rate**: Bank Indonesia policy rate.
- **BPS**: Statistics Indonesia.
- **CPI/IHK**: consumer price index.
- **Deficit**: government spending minus revenue.
- **Fiscal impulse**: change in fiscal stance affecting aggregate demand.
- **GDP/PDB**: gross domestic product.
- **GNP/PNB**: gross national product.
- **ICP**: Indonesian Crude-oil Price.
- **Inflation**: sustained increase in general price level.
- **KEM-PPKF**: Macroeconomic Framework and Principles of Fiscal Policy.
- **M1**: narrow money.
- **M2**: broad money.
- **Net exports**: exports minus imports.
- **PNBP**: non-tax state revenue.
- **Primary balance**: fiscal balance excluding interest payments.
- **SBN**: Indonesian government securities.
- **TPT**: open unemployment rate.
- **Yield**: return on a bond, inversely related to bond price.
